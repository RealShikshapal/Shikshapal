Chapter 6: Perimeter and Area

Measuring Distances and Regions in Two Dimensions

Two Ways to Measure 📏

Imagine you're running around a park. The distance you travel around the boundary is the perimeter. But when you look down from above and see the entire grass area you're covering, that's the area. These are fundamentally different measurements, yet they're deeply connected!

You might have two plots of land with the same perimeter but different areas—or the same area but different perimeters! Understanding both helps us solve real problems: fencing a garden, carpeting a floor, or laying tiles on a patio.

Why Does This Matter?

Key Concept

Perimeter and area are tools for quantifying the physical world. Architects use them to design buildings, farmers use them to plan irrigation, engineers use them to calculate material costs, and artists use them to understand composition. These aren't just abstract concepts—they're practical tools that professionals use daily to solve real problems efficiently and accurately.

6.1: Perimeter - The Distance Around

What Is Perimeter?

Perimeter is the distance you travel when walking around the entire boundary of a closed figure. For polygons (shapes made of straight lines), it's simply the sum of all side lengths.

Think of it as laying string around the edge of a shape. How much string do you need?

Perimeter of a Rectangle

A rectangle has two pairs of equal sides. If the length is 12 cm and breadth is 8 cm:

1
Identify the sides: length = 12 cm, breadth = 8 cm
2
A rectangle has opposite sides equal: 12, 8, 12, 8
3
Add them up: 12 + 8 + 12 + 8 = 40 cm
4
Or use the formula: 2 × (length + breadth) = 2 × (12 + 8) = 2 × 20 = 40 cm
Result
Perimeter = 2 × (l + b)

Why This Formula Works: The "Why" Behind the Formula

We use 2 × (length + breadth) because:

  • There are TWO sides of length L (top and bottom)
  • There are TWO sides of breadth B (left and right)
  • Total: L + B + L + B = 2L + 2B = 2(L + B)

Perimeter of a Square

All four sides of a square are equal. If each side is 5 m:

Perimeter = 4 × side = 4 × 5 = 20 m

This makes sense: a square has 4 identical sides, so we multiply the side length by 4.

Perimeter of a Triangle

Simply add all three sides together.

Triangle with sides 4 cm, 5 cm, 7 cm:

Perimeter = 4 + 5 + 7 = 16 cm

Regular Polygons and Perimeter

A regular polygon has all sides and all angles equal. Examples:

  • Equilateral triangle: 3 equal sides, so Perimeter = 3 × side
  • Regular pentagon: 5 equal sides, so Perimeter = 5 × side
  • Regular hexagon: 6 equal sides, so Perimeter = 6 × side

General formula: For any regular polygon with N sides of length S:

Perimeter = N × S

Application: Real-Life Perimeter Problems

Example 1: Akshi wants to put lace around a tablecloth that is 3 m long and 2 m wide. How much lace does she need?

Solution: Perimeter = 2 × (3 + 2) = 2 × 5 = 10 m

Example 2: Usha runs around a square park of side 75 m three times. What total distance did she run?

Solution: Perimeter of one lap = 4 × 75 = 300 m. Three laps = 3 × 300 = 900 m

Running Tracks and Competitive Fairness

On running tracks with inner and outer lanes, runners on outer lanes have a longer perimeter to cover. To make races fair, runners on outer lanes are given a head start! This is a beautiful real-world application of perimeter mathematics.

Perimeter Illusions: Straight vs. Diagonal

Consider a shape made of straight grid lines versus diagonal lines. Surprisingly, diagonals are longer than straight segments between the same points!

If you have a shape with some sides being diagonal on a grid:

  • Each straight unit on the grid = 1 unit
  • Each diagonal unit on the grid = √2 ≈ 1.414 units

This is why Toshi was right in the chapter problem—the diagonal path is longer than expected!

6.2: Area - The Surface Covered

What Is Area?

Area is the measure of the region enclosed by a closed figure. Imagine painting the entire interior of a shape—the area tells you how much paint you'd need.

Area is measured in square units: cm², m², etc. One square unit is a 1 × 1 square.

Area of a Rectangle

The most intuitive way to understand area is through rectangles. If you arrange 1 × 1 unit squares in a rectangle:

1
A rectangle 5 units long and 3 units wide
2
Can be filled with unit squares in 5 columns and 3 rows
3
Total unit squares = 5 × 3 = 15
Result
Area of rectangle = length × breadth = 5 × 3 = 15 square units

Area of a Square

Since a square has all sides equal (length = breadth = side):

Area of square = side × side = side²

Example: A square with side 4 m has area = 4 × 4 = 16 m²

Area and Perimeter Are Independent!

Here's a crucial insight: Two shapes can have the same perimeter but different areas, or the same area but different perimeters.

Example: Consider rectangles with perimeter 24 units:

  • 6 × 6 square: Area = 36 sq units
  • 8 × 4 rectangle: Area = 32 sq units
  • 10 × 2 rectangle: Area = 20 sq units

Same perimeter, different areas! This shows they're independent measurements.

Why Squares Give Maximum Area

Given a fixed perimeter, which shape encloses the most area? A square!

This is why:

  • Perimeter of all these shapes = 24 units
  • 6 × 6 square: Area = 36 (maximum)
  • 8 × 4 rectangle: Area = 32
  • 10 × 2 rectangle: Area = 20
  • 12 × 0 degenerate: Area = 0 (minimum)

The closer to a square (more balanced length and width), the larger the area for a given perimeter. This principle is used in design and engineering!

Estimating Area Using Grid Paper

For irregular shapes, use the following convention on grid paper:

  • Each fully enclosed unit square = 1 sq unit
  • Half or more of a square inside the shape = count it as 1 sq unit
  • Less than half = ignore it
  • Exactly half = count as 0.5 sq units
Why Squares Are Best for Measuring Area

You might wonder: Why not use circles or triangles for area units instead of squares?

Key reason: Shapes need to tessellate (fit together perfectly) to measure area without gaps or overlaps.

  • Squares fit together perfectly with no gaps—ideal for measurement
  • Circles don't—they always leave gaps between them
  • Triangles can work but are less efficient

This practical consideration is why we universally use square units for area measurement!

Real-Life Application: Tiling a Floor

Problem: A floor is 5 m long and 4 m wide. A square carpet of side 3 m is laid on it. What area remains untiled?

Solution:

  • Area of floor = 5 × 4 = 20 m²
  • Area of carpet = 3 × 3 = 9 m²
  • Untiled area = 20 - 9 = 11 m²

6.3: Area of a Triangle

The Discovery: Triangles Are Half-Rectangles

Take any rectangle and draw a diagonal. It divides the rectangle into TWO identical triangles! This reveals the formula:

1
Start with a rectangle of length L and breadth B
2
Area of rectangle = L × B
3
Draw a diagonal—it splits the rectangle into two identical triangles
4
Each triangle has area = (L × B) ÷ 2
Result
Area of triangle = ½ × base × height

Understanding "Base" and "Height"

In the formula Area = ½ × base × height:

  • Base: Any side of the triangle (the side you're "standing on")
  • Height: The perpendicular distance from the opposite vertex to that base (NOT the slant side)

This is crucial: Height must be perpendicular to the base, not the slant side of the triangle!

Why the ½ Factor? The Deep Geometry

Why exactly ½? Consider how a triangle relates to rectangles:

Imagine a triangle with base B and height H inside a rectangle of dimensions B × H. The triangle's area is exactly half the rectangle's area!

Why? Because as you move up from the base, the triangle's width decreases linearly until it reaches a point at the top. On average, it fills only half the rectangle's width. This elegant relationship is why the ½ appears in the formula.

Mathematically: ∫₀ᴴ (B × h/H) dh = ½ × B × H

Application: Breaking Irregular Shapes into Rectangles and Triangles

Complex shapes can be divided into rectangles and triangles, then their areas calculated and added:

Example: An L-shaped figure can be split into two rectangles, or an irregular shape can be split into rectangles and triangles, making calculation simple.

Common Error Trap: Using Slant Height Instead of Perpendicular Height

WRONG: Using the slant side of a triangle as the height in the area formula

RIGHT: The height must be perpendicular (at 90°) to the base

Visual check: If you draw the height line, it should meet the base at a right angle. If not, you're using the wrong measurement!

Making Shapes More or Less

The 9-Square Challenge

Using 9 unit squares (fixed area), you can arrange them to create vastly different perimeters:

  • Square arrangement (3 × 3): Perimeter = 12 units (minimum)
  • Line arrangement (1 × 9): Perimeter = 20 units (maximum)
  • Various other shapes: 14, 16, 18 units possible

The Key Insight

For a fixed area, shapes that are more "spread out" have larger perimeters. Compact shapes (closer to squares) have smaller perimeters. This is why:

  • Animals curl up to conserve heat (smaller perimeter, less surface exposure)
  • Circular designs are efficient for storage (most area for given perimeter)
  • Architects balance compactness and usable space
The Isoperimetric Problem

One of the most famous problems in mathematics: Among all shapes with the same perimeter, which has the largest area?

Answer: A circle!

For a given perimeter P, a circle encloses more area than any other shape. This is why:

  • Drums and plates are circular
  • Planets are spherical (2D version of circle)
  • Optimizing space with a fixed boundary often leads to circular designs

This deep mathematical principle guides engineering and design decisions!

Adding Squares Changes Both Perimeter and Area

When you attach one unit square to an existing figure:

  • Area always increases by 1 unit
  • Perimeter changes depending on WHERE you attach it:
    • Attach to a corner: Perimeter increases by 2
    • Attach to the middle of a side: Perimeter stays the same
    • Attach along an entire shared side: Perimeter decreases
Why Perimeter Changes Depend on Attachment Position

When you attach a new square:

To a corner: You add 3 new exposed sides and hide 0 old sides. Net change: +3 - 1 = +2 (but actually counted as exposing 2 full new sides and removing 1 = +1...)

Actually, the perimeter change is more nuanced: the number of newly exposed edges minus the number of hidden edges. This creates a beautiful optimization problem in discrete geometry!

Flexible Geometry: Tangrams

The Surprising Property

Tangram pieces can be rearranged to form different shapes—a square or a rectangle—all with the SAME total area but DIFFERENT perimeters!

This beautifully demonstrates that area and perimeter are independent properties.

Comparing Properties

When the 7 tangram pieces form:

  • A square: Minimal perimeter for the given area
  • A rectangle: Larger perimeter (same area, but more elongated)

The formula relationship: The perimeters differ precisely because one shape is more compact (square) while the other is more stretched (rectangle).

Common Error Trap: Mixing Up Area and Perimeter

WRONG: "This rectangle is bigger because its perimeter is larger" or "They have the same area, so they must have the same perimeter"

RIGHT: Area and perimeter measure different things and are independent.

Memory trick: Perimeter is about the OUTLINE (how far you walk around), Area is about the INSIDE (how much it covers).

Check yourself: A 10 × 1 rectangle has perimeter 22 but area 10. A 4 × 3 rectangle has perimeter 14 but area 12. Same perimeter isn't the same area!

Test Your Understanding

Work through these questions to assess your mastery of Perimeter and Area concepts.

Level 1: Building Foundations

Q1: Find the perimeter of a rectangle with length 8 m and breadth 5 m.

Answer: 26 m

Perimeter = 2 × (length + breadth) = 2 × (8 + 5) = 2 × 13 = 26 m

Q2: What is the area of a square with side 6 cm?

Answer: 36 cm²

Area of square = side × side = 6 × 6 = 36 cm²

Q3: Find the area of a triangle with base 10 cm and height 6 cm.

Answer: 30 cm²

Area of triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 10 × 6 = ½ × 60 = 30 cm²

Level 2: Applying Knowledge

Q4: A floor is 6 m long and 5 m wide. A square carpet of side 3 m is placed on it. What area remains exposed?

Answer: 21 m²

Area of floor = 6 × 5 = 30 m²

Area of carpet = 3 × 3 = 9 m²

Exposed area = 30 - 9 = 21 m²

Q5: Two rectangles have the same area (24 sq units). One is 6 × 4 and the other is 8 × 3. Do they have the same perimeter?

Answer: NO

Both have area 24 sq units. But:

Perimeter of 6 × 4 = 2 × (6 + 4) = 20 units

Perimeter of 8 × 3 = 2 × (8 + 3) = 22 units

Same area does NOT mean same perimeter!

Q6: What is the perimeter of a triangle with sides 7 cm, 8 cm, and 9 cm?

Answer: 24 cm

Perimeter = sum of all sides = 7 + 8 + 9 = 24 cm

Level 3: Deep Mastery

Q7: An L-shaped figure consists of two rectangles: one 8 × 3 and another 4 × 2 (arranged in an L). What is the total area?

Answer: 32 square units

Area of first rectangle = 8 × 3 = 24 sq units

Area of second rectangle = 4 × 2 = 8 sq units

Total area = 24 + 8 = 32 sq units

Q8: A farmer has a rectangular field 20 m × 15 m. He fences it with 3 rounds of rope. How much rope does he need?

Answer: 210 m

Perimeter = 2 × (20 + 15) = 2 × 35 = 70 m

For 3 rounds = 3 × 70 = 210 m

Q9: You have 9 unit squares. Arrange them in a square (3 × 3). What is the perimeter? Now arrange them in a line (1 × 9). What is the new perimeter? Why the difference?

Answer: 12 units (square) vs. 20 units (line)

Square (3 × 3): Perimeter = 4 × 3 = 12 units

Line (1 × 9): Perimeter = 2 × (1 + 9) = 20 units

Why? Compact shapes have smaller perimeters. The square is highly compact, while the line is extremely stretched out. Same area (9 sq units) but very different perimeters!
