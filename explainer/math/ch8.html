Chapter 8: Playing with Constructions

Master ruler and compass to draw circles, construct perpendiculars, and create geometric shapes with precision

The Hook: From Freehand Sketches to Perfect Drawings

Have you ever tried to draw a perfect circle by hand? Or tried to construct a square that looks actually square? With just a pencil, it's almost impossible to be accurate. But give an architect a ruler and compass, and they can construct buildings that stand for centuries. A civil engineer can mark roads with perfect right angles. A jeweler can create symmetric designs with precision.

The compass and ruler are not just tools—they're gateways to understanding how geometry works at its deepest level. They force you to think step-by-step, to plan before you act, and to verify that your logic is sound. This chapter teaches you the language of geometric construction.

The Feynman Bridge: Understanding Geometric Instruments

Key Insight A compass does one thing brilliantly: it marks all points at a fixed distance from a center. This is the definition of a circle. A ruler does two things: it connects points with straight lines and measures distances. Together, these simple tools reveal the hidden structure of geometry.

Every construction in this chapter begins with a question: "What points must satisfy these conditions?" For example, "All points 5 cm from point P form a circle of radius 5 cm." Once you answer that question, the construction becomes logical and inevitable.

Logic Ladder: The Circle—The Foundation of All Constructions

1

Mark a Center Point

Choose any point P on your paper. This will be the center of your circle. A circle is defined as "all points equidistant from a center."

2

Set the Compass to a Radius

Adjust the compass so the distance between the needle and pencil equals your desired radius (e.g., 4 cm). Place the needle at point P.

3

Rotate to Trace the Circle

Keep the needle fixed at P and rotate the pencil around it. Every point marked is exactly the same distance from P. Together, they form a circle.

4

Verify Your Work

Measure several points on your circle. All should be equidistant from center P. If they are, you've successfully constructed a circle based on geometric principles.

Deep-Dive: Why Constructing a Square Requires a Right Angle

A square must satisfy two properties: (1) All sides are equal in length, and (2) All angles are 90°. This means you can't just draw four equal sides—you must ensure they meet at right angles.

The Construction Logic:
Step 1: Draw one side PQ with a ruler (length = 6 cm).
Step 2: At point P, construct a line perpendicular to PQ using a compass. This line will contain point S such that ∠SPQ = 90°.
Step 3: Mark point S on the perpendicular such that PS = 6 cm (same as PQ).
Step 4: Repeat at Q to mark point R such that QR = 6 cm and QR is perpendicular to PQ.
Step 5: Verify: RS should equal 6 cm and all angles should be 90°.

Why This Works: By ensuring each side is equal and each angle is 90°, you automatically create a square. The right angle is the key—it guarantees the shape closes perfectly.

Deep-Dive: The Perpendicular Bisector—Finding Points Equidistant from Two Points

Imagine two points A and B. Is there a point that is equidistant (same distance) from both A and B? Yes—in fact, there are infinitely many such points, and they all lie on a line called the perpendicular bisector of AB.

The Logic: If you take a compass and draw a circle of any radius centered at A, then draw another circle of the same radius centered at B, they intersect at two points. Both intersection points are equidistant from A and B! The line connecting these two points is the perpendicular bisector.

The Construction:
1. Draw line segment AB.
2. Open the compass to more than half the length of AB.
3. With center A, draw an arc above and below AB.
4. With the same radius and center B, draw arcs intersecting the first pair.
5. Connect the two intersection points. This line is the perpendicular bisector.

Applications: Finding the center of a circle, constructing a rectangle, locating points equidistant from two reference points (like city planners finding a location equidistant from two neighborhoods).

Deep-Dive: Constructing Angles (60°, 45°, 30°, 90°)

Angles can be constructed using only a compass and ruler. The most fundamental angles are 60° and 90°, from which others are derived.

Constructing 60°: An equilateral triangle has three 60° angles. To construct it: (1) Draw a line segment AB. (2) Open the compass to length AB. (3) With center A, draw an arc above AB. (4) With center B and same radius, draw another arc intersecting the first. (5) Connect this intersection to A and B. Angle A = 60°.

Constructing 90°: Draw a perpendicular bisector of a line segment. The perpendicular meets the segment at 90°. Alternatively, construct a semicircle (circle with center at the midpoint of a diameter). Any angle inscribed in a semicircle is 90°.

Constructing 45°: Construct a 90° angle, then bisect it. (Bisecting means dividing into two equal parts using a compass.)

Constructing 30°: Construct a 60° angle, then bisect it.

The Logic: Every construction is based on the principle: "Mark all points satisfying a condition." For 60°, all points on the arc of a circle centered at A with radius AB define a specific angle when connected to A and B.

Deep-Dive: Regular Hexagon and Equilateral Triangle Inscribed in a Circle

Regular Hexagon Inscribed in a Circle: A regular hexagon is six equilateral triangles arranged around a center. Each interior angle is 120°, and the vertices divide the circle into six equal arcs.

Construction:
1. Draw a circle with center O and radius r.
2. Mark any point A on the circle.
3. With compass set to radius r (same as circle's radius), place the center at A and mark an arc on the circle. Call this point B.
4. Repeat from B, C, D, E, until you have six points evenly spaced around the circle.
5. Connect consecutive points with a ruler.

Why It Works: The six points are separated by 60° arcs. The side length of the inscribed hexagon equals the circle's radius—a beautiful relationship!

Equilateral Triangle Inscribed in a Circle: Take every other vertex of the hexagon. You now have three points evenly spaced (120° apart) on the circle, forming an equilateral triangle.

Key Insight: Regular polygons can be constructed by dividing a circle into equal parts using arcs. The radius of the circle becomes a crucial measurement.

Common Error Trap: Construction Pitfalls

Trap 1: "I'll keep the same compass opening for all steps, even when I need different radii."
Why it's wrong: Each step in a construction often requires a specific radius based on the line segment or angle you're working with. Changing the compass opening is part of the construction process, not cheating.
Fix: Always adjust the compass to the exact measurement needed for each step. Use the original line segments to calibrate.

Trap 2: "I'll draw light arcs first, then erase them later. No one needs to see them."
Why it's wrong: In mathematical constructions, the arcs are evidence of your thinking. They show that you understand why a point is where it is. Erasing them hides the logic.
Fix: Keep all construction lines visible. Use a pencil lightly so arcs don't dominate, but never erase them. In formal geometry, these auxiliary lines show proof.

Trap 3: "I can eyeball whether this angle is 90°. I don't need to verify it."
Why it's wrong: "Eyeballing" defeats the entire purpose of construction. The point is to create shapes that are provably correct, not approximately correct.
Fix: Always verify angles and distances. If constructing a rectangle, check that all four angles are 90° using a compass or protractor.

Socratic Quiz: Test Your Construction Understanding

These questions probe your understanding of construction principles. Think deeply before peeking at answers.

Level 1: Basic Circle and Instrument Knowledge

Question 1: What is a radius, and how does it relate to drawing a circle with a compass?

Show Answer

Answer: The radius is the distance from the center of a circle to any point on the circle. It's the distance you set on the compass between the needle and pencil. All points on the circle are exactly one radius away from the center.

Question 2: You need to draw a circle of radius 5 cm. Describe the steps using a compass.

Show Answer

Answer:
1. Measure 5 cm on your ruler between the compass needle and pencil.
2. Mark a center point on your paper and place the needle there.
3. Keep the needle fixed and rotate the pencil around it to trace the circle.

Question 3: What two properties must a square have?

Show Answer

Answer: (1) All four sides must be equal in length, and (2) All four angles must be 90°.

Note: If only one property holds, it's not a square. A rectangle has property 2 but not 1. A rhombus has property 1 but not 2.

Level 2: Planning and Geometric Reasoning

Question 1: You're constructing a square with side length 5 cm. What must you do immediately after drawing the first side PQ = 5 cm?

Show Answer

Answer: Construct a line perpendicular to PQ at point P. This perpendicular will contain the second side PS. The perpendicular ensures the angle at P is 90°.

Explanation: Without the perpendicular, you can't guarantee a right angle. The perpendicular is the key to constructing a square rather than an arbitrary quadrilateral.

Question 2: You want to find a point that is equidistant from two given points A and B. What is this line called, and how do you construct it?

Show Answer

Answer: This is the perpendicular bisector of line segment AB.

Construction: (1) Open compass to more than half the length of AB. (2) With center A, draw an arc above and below AB. (3) With the same radius and center B, draw intersecting arcs. (4) Connect the two intersection points. Any point on this line is equidistant from A and B.

Question 3: A rectangle has sides 8 cm and 4 cm. You want to construct a square inside it such that the square's center coincides with the rectangle's center. What should be the side length of the square?

Show Answer

Answer: The side length should be 4 cm (equal to the shorter side of the rectangle).

Explanation: For the square to fit symmetrically inside with the same center, its maximum side length is limited by the shorter dimension of the rectangle. Place it centered so there's equal spacing (2 cm) on each side.

Level 3: Complex Constructions and Verification

Question 1: Explain how to construct a regular hexagon inscribed in a circle of radius 5 cm.

Show Answer

Answer:
1. Draw a circle with center O and radius 5 cm.
2. Mark a point A on the circle.
3. Set the compass to 5 cm (the radius).
4. With center A, mark point B on the circle.
5. With center B and same radius, mark point C on the circle.
6. Repeat until you have six evenly spaced points (A, B, C, D, E, F).
7. Connect consecutive points to form the hexagon.

Key Fact: The side length of the inscribed regular hexagon equals the circle's radius.

Question 2: You need to construct a rectangle where one side is 6 cm and the diagonal is 10 cm. What steps would you follow?

Show Answer

Answer:
1. Draw the base CD = 6 cm.
2. Construct a perpendicular to CD at point C.
3. Set the compass to 10 cm (the diagonal length).
4. With center D, draw an arc that intersects the perpendicular at point B.
5. Point B is 10 cm from D (the diagonal) and lies on the perpendicular (ensuring the right angle).
6. Complete the rectangle by drawing perpendiculars at B and D to form point A.

Verification: Measure: CD should be 6 cm, and diagonal DB should be 10 cm. All angles should be 90°.

Question 3: You want to construct an angle of 45°. Which two angles would you combine, or which angle would you bisect?

Show Answer

Answer: Construct a 90° angle and bisect it (cut it in half).

Why: 45° = 90° ÷ 2. To bisect, use a compass to mark equal distances along the two rays of the 90° angle, then use the perpendicular bisector method to find the angle bisector.

Alternative: You could also bisect a 60° angle and add a 30° angle, but bisecting 90° is simpler.
