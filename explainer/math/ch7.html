Chapter 7: Fractions

Understanding parts of a whole, equivalence, and operations with fractional units

The Hook: Why Fractions Matter

Imagine you're sharing a pizza with friends. How do you describe "half a pizza"? Or if you eat 3 slices out of 8, what fraction is that? Fractions are the mathematical language for expressing equal parts of a whole—and they're everywhere in real life: cooking, splitting bills, measuring distances, and even time on a clock.

The beauty of fractions is that they show us how to think about quantities that don't fit into whole numbers. By mastering fractions, you unlock a deeper understanding of how numbers work and how to divide things fairly and precisely.

The Feynman Bridge: Seeing the Logic

Key Insight A fraction is not just a number—it's a story about equal parts. When we write 3/4, we're saying: "Divide one whole into 4 equal pieces (the denominator tells us the size of each piece), and take 3 of those pieces (the numerator tells us how many we take)."

The denominator (bottom number) defines the fractional unit—the size of each equal share. The numerator (top number) tells us how many of those units we have. This is the foundation for everything else in fractions.

Logic Ladder: From Whole to Fraction

1

Start with a Whole

Take one whole unit (a pizza, a cake, a length of rope). This represents 1 or the complete whole.

2

Divide into Equal Parts

Cut your whole into equal pieces. If you cut a pizza into 4 equal slices, each slice is 1/4 of the pizza. The denominator 4 tells us the number of equal parts.

3

Count How Many You Have

If you take 3 of those 4 slices, you have 3/4. The numerator 3 tells us how many equal parts we're selecting.

4

Express as a Fraction

Write the fraction as: numerator/denominator = 3/4. This is your mathematical representation of "three-quarters."

Deep-Dive: Why Are Equivalent Fractions the Same?

Here's a surprising truth: 2/4, 3/6, 4/8, and 1/2 are all the same amount! Why? Because they describe the same length on a number line and the same share of a whole.

The Logic: If you fold a piece of paper in half and shade one part, you have 1/2. If you then fold it in half again (creating 4 parts total) and shade 2 parts, you still have the same shaded area. So 1/2 = 2/4.

The Rule: When you multiply both the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number, you create an equivalent fraction. For example:

  • 1/2 × 2/2 = 2/4 (multiply both top and bottom by 2)
  • 1/2 × 3/3 = 3/6 (multiply both top and bottom by 3)
  • 2/3 × 5/5 = 10/15 (multiply both top and bottom by 5)

This works because you're not changing the actual value—you're just expressing it with a different fractional unit.

Deep-Dive: How to Compare Fractions

Comparing fractions at first seems confusing. Is 3/5 bigger than 2/3? To answer, we use equivalent fractions to create a common denominator—a denominator that appears in both fractions.

The Method (Brahmagupta's approach):

  1. Find a common denominator: For 3/5 and 2/3, multiply 5 × 3 = 15. This is a denominator both fractions can use.
  2. Convert to equivalent fractions: 3/5 = 9/15 (multiply by 3/3) and 2/3 = 10/15 (multiply by 5/5).
  3. Compare the numerators: Now compare 9/15 and 10/15. Since 9 < 10, we have 3/5 < 2/3.

Quick Tip: If two fractions have the same denominator, the one with the larger numerator is larger. If they have the same numerator, the one with the smaller denominator is larger (because fewer pieces to divide = bigger pieces).

Deep-Dive: Addition and Subtraction of Fractions

Why can't you just add 1/3 + 1/5 by adding numerators and denominators to get 2/8? Because you'd be adding pieces of different sizes—like combining thirds with fifths!

The Logic: You can only add or subtract fractions when they have the same fractional unit (same denominator). If they don't, first convert them to equivalent fractions with a common denominator.

Brahmagupta's Method for Addition:

  1. Find equivalent fractions with a common denominator
  2. Add the numerators; keep the denominator the same
  3. Simplify the result if possible

Example: 1/3 + 1/5
Common denominator: 3 × 5 = 15
1/3 = 5/15 and 1/5 = 3/15
5/15 + 3/15 = 8/15

For Subtraction: The same method applies. First convert to a common denominator, then subtract the numerators.

Deep-Dive: Mixed Fractions vs. Improper Fractions

Improper Fractions: When the numerator is equal to or greater than the denominator (e.g., 7/4, 5/5), the fraction is "improper." This doesn't mean it's wrong—it just means the fraction represents one whole or more than one whole.

Mixed Fractions: These combine a whole number with a fraction (e.g., 1 3/4). They're easier to visualize in everyday situations.

How to Convert:
Improper to Mixed: Divide the numerator by the denominator. The quotient is the whole number, and the remainder becomes the new numerator.
Example: 7/4 ÷ 4 = 1 remainder 3, so 7/4 = 1 3/4

Mixed to Improper: Multiply the whole number by the denominator and add the numerator.
Example: 2 3/5 = (2 × 5 + 3)/5 = 13/5

Common Error Trap: Fraction Misconceptions

Trap 1: "3/8 is bigger than 1/2 because 3 > 1."
Why it's wrong: You're only comparing numerators and ignoring the denominator entirely. 1/2 = 4/8, and 4/8 > 3/8.
Fix: Always convert to the same denominator before comparing.

Trap 2: "1/4 + 1/4 = 2/8" (adding numerators AND denominators).
Why it's wrong: When you add same-sized pieces (fourths + fourths), the denominator stays the same. You only add numerators.
Fix: 1/4 + 1/4 = 2/4 = 1/2. The denominator never changes when adding like fractions.

Trap 3: "1/3 + 1/5 = 2/8" (ignoring the need for common denominators).
Why it's wrong: You can't add thirds and fifths directly—they're different-sized pieces. You must first convert to a common unit.
Fix: Find a common denominator (15), convert to 5/15 + 3/15 = 8/15.

Socratic Quiz: Test Your Understanding

Work through these questions to deepen your grasp of fractions. Try to answer each level before looking at the explanation.

Level 1: Foundational Understanding

Question 1: A chocolate bar is divided into 8 equal pieces. You eat 3 pieces. What fraction of the chocolate bar did you eat?

Show Answer

Answer: 3/8

Explanation: The denominator (8) represents the total number of equal pieces. The numerator (3) represents how many pieces you ate. So the fraction is 3/8.

Question 2: Which fraction represents the shaded part of this shape if 1 whole = 4 equal pieces and 2 pieces are shaded?

Show Answer

Answer: 2/4 (or simplified as 1/2)

Explanation: The whole is divided into 4 equal pieces, and 2 are shaded. So the fraction is 2/4. Since 2/4 = 1/2, both answers are correct.

Question 3: Mark the fraction 1/2 on a number line from 0 to 1. At which point should it go?

Show Answer

Answer: 1/2 should be marked at the midpoint of the number line, exactly halfway between 0 and 1.

Explanation: 1/2 means one out of two equal parts. So on a number line from 0 to 1, it divides that distance into two equal halves.

Level 2: Applying Equivalence and Comparison

Question 1: Are 2/3 and 4/6 equivalent fractions? Explain why or why not.

Show Answer

Answer: Yes, they are equivalent.

Explanation: When we multiply 2/3 by 2/2, we get (2 × 2)/(3 × 2) = 4/6. Both fractions represent the same amount on a number line and the same share of a whole.

Question 2: Compare these fractions: 3/5 and 2/4. Which is larger?

Show Answer

Answer: 3/5 is larger.

Explanation: Convert to a common denominator (20). 3/5 = 12/20 and 2/4 = 10/20. Since 12/20 > 10/20, we know 3/5 > 2/4.

Question 3: Convert the improper fraction 9/4 to a mixed number.

Show Answer

Answer: 2 1/4

Explanation: Divide 9 by 4: 9 ÷ 4 = 2 remainder 1. So 9/4 = 2 1/4 (two wholes and one-fourth).

Level 3: Operations and Problem-Solving

Question 1: Add these fractions: 1/3 + 1/6. Simplify your answer.

Show Answer

Answer: 1/2

Explanation: Find a common denominator (6). Convert: 1/3 = 2/6. Now add: 2/6 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 (simplified).

Question 2: You have 3/4 of a pizza. You eat 1/8 of the pizza. How much pizza is left?

Show Answer

Answer: 5/8 of the pizza

Explanation: Set up the subtraction: 3/4 - 1/8. Convert to a common denominator (8): 3/4 = 6/8. Now subtract: 6/8 - 1/8 = 5/8.

Question 3: A recipe calls for 2 1/2 cups of flour. If you want to double the recipe, how much flour do you need?

Show Answer

Answer: 5 cups of flour

Explanation: First, convert 2 1/2 to an improper fraction: 2 1/2 = 5/2. Double it: 5/2 × 2 = 10/2 = 5 cups.
