The Other Side of Zero

Chapter 10: Integers

The Hook: What Happens Below Zero?

You know the counting numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4, ... You know about zero. You know about fractions like 1/2 and 3/4. But what about a floor below the ground? A temperature below freezing? A bank balance in debt? A year before the Common Era?

These real-world situations point to something profound: there are numbers that go the opposite direction from the counting numbers. They go to the left on the number line, below zero. These are negative numbers, and together with positive numbers and zero, they form the complete set of integers.

The Feynman Bridge: Why Negative Numbers Matter

Concept

For centuries, mathematicians resisted negative numbers. But the moment we ask, "What's the opposite of going up?" or "What's the opposite of gaining money?" we need them. Negative numbers are not abstract—they describe real situations: debts, depths, temperatures, directions.

The key insight: integers form a complete number system where every positive number has a negative opposite. Together, they allow us to describe motion in both directions, changes in both directions, and balances that go both ways.

Logic Ladder 1: From Positive Numbers to Integers

1

The Number Line Extends

You learned the number line starting at 0 going right: 0, 1, 2, 3, ... But the number line actually extends to the left of 0 as well: ..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ... This extended line represents all integers.

2

Positive vs. Negative Numbers

A positive number has a '+' sign (though we usually write just "5" instead of "+5"). A negative number has a '–' sign (written as "-5"). Zero is neither positive nor negative—it's the boundary, the reference point.

3

Bela's Building: A Real-World Model

Imagine a building with floors above and below ground. The ground floor is Floor 0. Going up: Floor +1, +2, +3, ... Going down: Floor -1, -2, -3, ... Each floor number tells you where you are relative to ground level. This concrete model makes negative numbers tangible.

4

Opposites and Inverses

Every positive integer has a negative opposite. The opposite of +5 is -5. The opposite of -3 is +3. These opposites are called additive inverses—when you add them together, you get zero: (+5) + (-5) = 0.

Deep Dive: The Lift Model and Button Presses

Bela's Building uses a lift with two buttons: '+' (go up) and '–' (go down). Pressing '+' three times (or pressing '+3') takes you three floors up. Pressing '–' twice takes you two floors down.

Key relationships:

  • Button press '+3' from Floor 0 takes you to Floor +3
  • Button press '–2' from Floor 0 takes you to Floor -2
  • Button press '+2' from Floor -1 takes you to Floor +1 (because -1 + 2 = 1)
  • Button press '–3' from Floor +2 takes you to Floor -1 (because 2 - 3 = -1)

Why this model is powerful: It separates starting position from movement. The same button press has different results depending where you start. This mirrors real integers: the same debt (negative number) has different meanings depending on your current balance.

Connecting to the Number Line: The lift model naturally extends to the number line. Moving right (up) is positive. Moving left (down) is negative. The number line becomes a tool for visualizing addition and subtraction.

Logic Ladder 2: Comparing and Ordering Integers

1

The Position Rule on the Number Line

On the number line, numbers to the right are larger than numbers to the left. So -1 > -3 (because -1 is to the right of -3), and -2 < 5 (because -2 is to the left of 5). Use the number line, not intuition.

2

Negative Numbers Get Larger (less negative)

This trips up many students: -2 > -5, even though we think of 2 and 5 as numbers where 5 > 2. The key: -2 is closer to zero and to the right on the number line. Think of floors: Floor -2 is higher than Floor -5.

3

Positive Numbers Are Always Greater

Any positive integer is greater than any negative integer: 1 > -100, 0 > -5, etc. Zero is greater than all negative integers but less than all positive integers. This establishes a complete ordering.

4

Finding the Lowest and Highest

Given several integers, the lowest is the one furthest to the left on the number line. The highest is furthest to the right. Example: among -7, -3, 2, and 5, the lowest is -7, and the highest is 5.

Deep Dive: Adding Integers—Two Models

Model 1: The Lift (Building Floors)

Start at a floor, press buttons (positive = up, negative = down). Example: Start at Floor +2, press -3. You go from +2 down three floors: +2 → +1 → 0 → -1. So (+2) + (-3) = -1.

Model 2: The Number Line

Start at a number on the line. Move right for positive, left for negative. Same example: Start at +2, move 3 steps left. You land at -1. So (+2) + (-3) = -1.

Model 3: Credits and Debits (Money)

Think of positive numbers as money you have (credits) and negative numbers as money you owe (debits). If you have +50 rupees and owe -30 rupees, your net position is +50 + (-30) = +20 rupees.

The Pattern: All three models give the same answer because they represent the same underlying principle: combining two movements or two quantities results in a net position.

Key Rules for Adding:

  • Positive + Positive = Positive (further right)
  • Negative + Negative = Negative (further left)
  • Positive + Negative = depends on magnitudes (whichever has larger absolute value wins the sign)

Logic Ladder 3: Subtracting Integers

1

Subtraction as Finding a Movement

Instead of "starting floor + movement," think of subtraction as: "What movement gets me from start to end?" Example: From Floor +2 to Floor -1. We need: (+2) + ? = -1, so ? = -3. Thus, (+2) – (+3) = -1.

2

Subtracting Negatives = Adding Positives

This is the key insight students must grasp: (+3) – (-2) = (+3) + (+2) = +5. Why? Because subtracting a debt is like gaining money. If you owe someone -2 and they forgive the debt, it's like gaining +2.

3

The Rule: Subtract = Add the Opposite

a – b = a + (-b). To subtract any integer, add its opposite. So: 5 – 3 = 5 + (-3) = 2. And: 5 – (-3) = 5 + 3 = 8. This single rule handles all cases.

4

Using the Number Line to Check

For any subtraction, move on the number line. (+4) – (-3): Start at +4, move right 3 steps (adding the opposite of -3). You land at +7. This visual confirmation builds confidence.

Deep Dive: Integers in Real Life

Temperature: A thermometer shows positive temps (above freezing, 0°C) and negative temps (below freezing). If it's -5°C and rises by 8°C, the new temperature is -5 + 8 = +3°C. This is integer addition.

Altitude (Height): Mountains are positive (above sea level). The Dead Sea is about -400m (below sea level). If you climb from -400m to +2000m, you've climbed 2000 – (-400) = 2400m. Integer subtraction.

Bank Balance: A credit (positive) adds to your balance. A withdrawal or debt (negative) subtracts. Starting balance -100 rupees (you owe), you earn +500. New balance: -100 + 500 = +400 rupees (you now have 400).

Timeline (BCE to CE): The year -100 (100 BCE) is 100 years before year 0 (the reference). The year +500 (500 CE) is 500 years after. The time between them is: 500 – (-100) = 600 years.

Why Integers Matter: These scenarios would be impossible or confusing using only positive numbers and zero. Negative numbers make the math consistent and powerful.

Common Error Trap

Mistake 1: "A negative number is smaller than its positive opposite."

Why it's wrong: -5 < 5, yes. But -5 is not "bigger in badness." On the number line, -5 is simply to the left of 5. The absolute values are equal (both 5 units from zero); the directions differ. A floor of -2 is not "smaller" than floor +2; it's just below ground instead of above.

Mistake 2: "-3 > -7 is false because 3 < 7."

Why it's wrong: This confuses the numbers with their absolute values. On the number line, -3 is to the right of -7, so -3 > -7 is TRUE. Think of it as floors: Floor -3 is higher (closer to ground) than Floor -7. Compare positions on the line, not the digits.

Mistake 3: "Subtracting a negative always means subtraction becomes harder."

Why it's wrong: Subtracting a negative actually gives you a larger answer (it's like adding a positive). Example: 5 – (-3) = 8, not 2. Forgetting a debt (subtracting a negative) is the same as gaining money (adding a positive).

Mistake 4: "The answer must always have the sign of the larger number."

Why it's wrong: In (-2) + (+8), the larger number is +8, and the answer is indeed +6. But in (-2) + (-8), there is no "larger positive"; the answer is -10 (both negative). The rule only applies to mixed-sign addition; for same-sign addition, the answer has that sign.

Socratic Quiz: Test Your Understanding

Level 1: Recognition (Basic)

Q1: Which of the following is an integer?

a) 3.5

b) -7

c) 1/2

d) Both b and the parts before

Show Answer

Answer: b) -7

Integers are whole numbers (positive, negative, or zero). -7 is a negative integer. 3.5 is a decimal (not an integer). 1/2 is a fraction (not an integer).

Q2: Which number is to the right of -3 on the number line?

a) -5

b) -4

c) -2

d) -6

Show Answer

Answer: c) -2

The number line goes: ... -6, -5, -4, -3, -2, -1, 0, ... To the right (toward 0 and beyond), -2 comes after -3. Numbers to the right are larger, so -2 > -3.

Q3: What is the opposite (additive inverse) of -8?

a) -8

b) 8

c) 0

d) 1/8

Show Answer

Answer: b) 8

The additive inverse of any number is what you add to it to get zero. -8 + 8 = 0. So the inverse of -8 is +8.

Q4: You start on Floor -2 and press the lift button to go up 5 floors. Which floor do you reach?

a) Floor -7

b) Floor +3

c) Floor -3

d) Floor 0

Show Answer

Answer: b) Floor +3

Starting floor + movement = result. (-2) + (+5) = +3. You go from -2 up five floors: -2 → -1 → 0 → +1 → +2 → +3.

Level 2: Application (Intermediate)

Q5: Evaluate: (+4) + (-7)

a) +11

b) +3

c) -3

d) -11

Show Answer

Answer: c) -3

Start at +4 and move 7 steps left (negative = left). +4 → +3 → +2 → +1 → 0 → -1 → -2 → -3. Or: we have +4 but owe 7, leaving a debt of 3, so -3.

Q6: Evaluate: (-5) – (-3)

a) -8

b) -2

c) +2

d) +8

Show Answer

Answer: b) -2

Subtract a negative = add the positive opposite. (-5) – (-3) = (-5) + (+3) = -2. You're on floor -5, and subtracting (removing) a debt of -3 is like gaining +3 floors, taking you to floor -2.

Q7: The temperature is -8°C. It rises by 12°C. What is the new temperature?

a) -20°C

b) -4°C

c) +4°C

d) +20°C

Show Answer

Answer: c) +4°C

(-8) + (+12) = +4. Start at -8, add 12: -8 → -7 → ... → 0 → +1 → +2 → +3 → +4.

Q8: Your bank account is -200 rupees (you owe 200). You earn 500 rupees. What is your new balance?

a) -700 rupees

b) -300 rupees

c) +300 rupees

d) +700 rupees

Show Answer

Answer: c) +300 rupees

(-200) + (+500) = +300. You owe 200 but earn 500, leaving a net credit of 300 rupees.

Level 3: Analysis (Advanced)

Q9: Order these integers from smallest to largest: -5, 0, -2, 3, -10, 1

Show Answer

Answer: -10, -5, -2, 0, 1, 3

The number line goes left to right: ..., -10, ..., -5, ..., -2, 0, 1, ..., 3, ... On a number line, leftmost is smallest, rightmost is largest. So the order is: -10 < -5 < -2 < 0 < 1 < 3.

Q10: From the year 100 BCE (written as -100), how many years pass to reach the year 200 CE (written as +200)?

Show Answer

Answer: 300 years

The time between two points on the number line is: (+200) – (-100) = (+200) + (+100) = +300. Or: start at -100, move to 0 (that's 100 years), then move to +200 (that's 200 more years), totaling 100 + 200 = 300 years.

Q11: True or False: The sum of an integer and its additive inverse is always 0.

Show Answer

Answer: True

By definition, the additive inverse of a is the number that when added to a gives 0. So a + (-a) = 0, always. Examples: 5 + (-5) = 0, (-7) + 7 = 0, 0 + 0 = 0.

Q12: A submarine starts at sea level (0), descends 150 meters (-150), then rises 80 meters. What is its final depth?

Show Answer

Answer: -70 meters (70 meters below sea level)

Start at 0. Descend 150: 0 + (-150) = -150. Rise 80: (-150) + (+80) = -70. The submarine is 70 meters below sea level.
