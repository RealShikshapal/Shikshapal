Mechanical Properties of Solids

Solids are rigid—they resist being pushed, pulled, twisted, or stretched. Yet no material is perfectly rigid.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Mechanical Properties of Solids. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Solids are rigid—they resist being pushed, pulled, twisted, or stretched. Yet no material is perfectly rigid. Apply enough force to a steel beam, and it bends. Even mountains deform under their own weight over geological timescales. Understanding how solids respond to forces reveals why materials fail, how engineers design structures, and what determines material strength. This chapter explores stress, strain, and elasticity.

Stress: Force Per Unit Area

When you push on an object, the effect depends not just on force magnitude but on how concentrated the force is. Push with a sharp point, and it penetrates; push with a flat hand, and it only hurts slightly. This distinction defines stress: force per unit area.

Stress = F/A

Stress is measured in pascals (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m². High stress means concentrated force; low stress means distributed force.

There are three types of stress:

  • Tensile stress: pulling force (stretching)
  • Compressive stress: pushing force (squeezing)
  • Shear stress: sideways force causing layers to slide relative to each other

A rope under tension experiences tensile stress. A column in a building experiences compressive stress. A material undergoing torsion experiences shear stress.

Strain: Proportional Deformation

Apply stress to a material, and it deforms. A rubber band stretches; a compressed spring compresses. Strain measures the fractional deformation:

Strain = (Change in length) / (Original length) = ΔL/L

Strain is dimensionless—it's a ratio. A strain of 0.01 means 1% deformation. Strain can be tensile (stretching increases length), compressive (squeezing decreases length), or shear (layers shift sideways).

The key insight: strain is relative. The same absolute stretch (1 cm) is significant for a 1-meter wire but negligible for a 100-meter cable. Strain accounts for this by considering proportions.

Hooke's Law: Linear Elasticity

For small stresses, stress and strain are proportional:

Stress = E × Strain

where E is the elastic modulus, a material property. This is Hooke's law for bulk deformation. For tensile/compressive stress, E is called Young's modulus.

Young's modulus has units of pressure (pascals). High Young's modulus means the material is stiff (difficult to stretch). Diamond has enormous Young's modulus; rubber has low Young's modulus.

Rearranging: Strain = Stress/E. Stiffer materials (higher E) produce less strain for the same stress.

The proportionality is linear for small deformations—double the stress, double the strain. But Hooke's law breaks down at large stresses. Eventually, the material deforms permanently (plastic deformation) or breaks.

Stress-Strain Curves: Material Behavior

Plotting stress versus strain reveals a material's full behavior:

  1. Elastic region (small stress): Strain is proportional to stress. Release the stress, and the material returns to original shape. The relationship is linear, slope = E.
  1. Yield point: Beyond this stress, Hooke's law fails. Stress and strain are no longer proportional.
  1. Plastic region (medium-large stress): Material deforms, but doesn't return to original shape when stress is removed. Releasing stress here leaves permanent deformation.
  1. Failure point: The stress at which the material breaks. This is the ultimate tensile strength.

The area under the curve represents energy: elastic energy (which is recovered) in the elastic region, and plastic energy (which causes permanent damage) in the plastic region.

Elastic Moduli: Quantifying Stiffness

Different types of stress have different moduli:

Young's modulus (Y): For tensile/compressive stress. Measures resistance to length change.

Bulk modulus (K): For uniform compression from all sides. Measures resistance to volume change. Materials with high bulk modulus are incompressible.

Shear modulus (G): For shear stress. Measures resistance to shape change (without volume change).

A material can have different values for each, reflecting its internal structure. Steel is stiff in tension (high Young's modulus) and very incompressible (high bulk modulus). Rubber is easily stretched (low Young's modulus) but barely compressible (high bulk modulus).

Applications of Elasticity

Understanding elasticity guides engineering design:

  • Bridge cables are chosen for high Young's modulus (minimal stretching under load)
  • Shock absorbers use materials with low modulus to dissipate impact energy
  • Pressure vessels require high bulk modulus to prevent rupture
  • Seismic dampers in buildings use plastic deformation to absorb earthquake energy

Concrete is strong in compression but weak in tension. Steel is strong in both. Reinforced concrete combines them: concrete resists compression, steel resists tension.

Elastic Potential Energy

When a material is elastically deformed, it stores energy. For a stretched wire:

PE = (1/2) × E × A × (Strain)² × Volume = (1/2) × Stress × Strain × Volume

This energy is released when the material returns to its original shape. This principle powers wind-up toys, catapults, and the recoil of arrows from a bow.

The maximum elastic energy a material can store before permanent damage is the area under the elastic region of the stress-strain curve—larger elastic regions mean tougher materials.

Socratic Questions

  1. Why does stress depend on the area over which force is applied, not just the magnitude of the force? Give examples where this distinction matters practically.
  1. A rubber band and a steel wire are both stretched by the same fraction (same strain). Which experiences more stress, and why?
  1. Below the yield point, a material obeys Hooke's law, but above it, stress-strain becomes nonlinear. What happens at the molecular level to cause this transition?
  1. Why can't all materials be made infinitely stiff (high Young's modulus)? What trade-offs exist in material design?
  1. A glass rod and a rubber rod, both of the same size, are subjected to the same tensile stress. Glass is stiffer but breaks suddenly. Rubber deforms plastically but survives. How do their stress-strain curves differ?
