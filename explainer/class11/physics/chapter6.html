System of Particles and Rotational Motion

Most objects aren't point particles—they have size and shape. A spinning top, a rolling wheel, a tumbling asteroid—these rotate as well as translate.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about System of Particles and Rotational Motion. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Most objects aren't point particles—they have size and shape. A spinning top, a rolling wheel, a tumbling asteroid—these rotate as well as translate. Understanding systems of many particles and rotational motion reveals how complex bodies move and what conserves in collisions. This chapter introduces the center of mass, rotational dynamics, and the beautiful symmetry between linear and rotational motion.

Center of Mass: The Average Position

Imagine a pencil falling and spinning through the air. Its individual atoms follow complex paths, but one special point—the center of mass—follows a simple parabolic trajectory, as if all mass were concentrated there. The center of mass is the average position of all mass in an object, weighted by how much mass is at each location.

For a symmetric object (like a sphere or cube), the center of mass is at the geometric center. For an irregular shape, it might be off to one side or even outside the object (like a ring or horseshoe). The center of mass depends on how mass is distributed.

Mathematically, if an object has particles at positions x₁, x₂, x₃... with masses m₁, m₂, m₃..., the center of mass is at: x_cm = (m₁x₁ + m₂x₂ + m₃x₃...) / (m₁ + m₂ + m₃...)

It's a weighted average position. For two equal masses, the center of mass is exactly halfway between them. For unequal masses, it's closer to the heavier mass.

Motion of the Center of Mass

Here's a profound insight: the center of mass of an isolated system moves as if all external forces act on it, regardless of internal forces or rotation.

When a person stands on a skateboard and jumps off, internal forces between the person and skateboard push them in opposite directions. But if there's no external force (on a frictionless surface), the center of mass remains at rest. The person moves one way, the skateboard the other, but their center of mass doesn't move.

This explains why you can't lift yourself by your bootstraps: your upward force on yourself equals your downward force on yourself (Newton's third law). Internal forces can't move the center of mass.

Linear Momentum of a System

The momentum of a system is the vector sum of all particles' momenta. Remarkably, it equals the total mass times the velocity of the center of mass: P_total = M_total × v_cm.

In an isolated system (no external forces), total momentum is conserved. This is the principle behind collisions and explosions. In a collision, momentum before equals momentum after, even though internal forces are enormous and velocities change drastically.

Rotational Motion: Analogies to Linear Motion

Just as translational motion is described by force, mass, and acceleration, rotational motion has analogous concepts:

  • Angular displacement (θ, in radians): like linear displacement
  • Angular velocity (ω): like linear velocity (how fast the angle changes)
  • Angular acceleration (α): like linear acceleration (how fast angular velocity changes)
  • Torque (τ): like force (causes angular acceleration)
  • Moment of inertia (I): like mass (resistance to angular acceleration)

The angular analog of F = ma is τ = Iα (torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration).

Moment of Inertia: Rotational Resistance

Moment of inertia measures how hard it is to spin something. It depends on mass and how far that mass is from the rotation axis. A dumbbell with weights far from the center has higher moment of inertia than the same mass concentrated near the center.

Mathematically, I = Σmᵢrᵢ², where rᵢ is the distance of each mass from the axis. Mass far from the axis contributes much more to moment of inertia (because of r²).

This explains why spinning ice skaters pull in their arms to spin faster: reducing the radius decreases moment of inertia, so angular velocity must increase to conserve angular momentum (like a spinning top).

Angular Momentum and Conservation

Just as linear momentum is conserved, angular momentum (L = Iω) is conserved in the absence of external torques.

A spinning figure skater demonstrates this. Arms extended: large moment of inertia, slow spin. Arms pulled in: small moment of inertia, fast spin. The angular momentum stays constant, so ω increases.

This principle applies to planets, atoms, and galaxies. Earth spins at constant angular momentum. If its moment of inertia somehow changed (it doesn't, but imagine it did), its spin rate would adjust to keep L constant.

Torque: The Rotational Force

Torque is the rotational analog of force. It's defined as τ = r × F, the cross product of position and force. Torque depends on force magnitude, distance from the axis, and the angle between them.

A door opens easily when you push far from the hinge (large r) but is hard to open when you push near the hinge (small r). This is why doorknobs are placed far from hinges—to maximize torque.

Even if you exert the same force in the same direction, pushing perpendicular to the door produces maximum torque. Pushing along the door's edge (parallel to its edge) produces zero torque.

Rolling Motion: Rotation Plus Translation

When a wheel rolls without slipping, it both rotates and translates. The velocity of the center is v, and the angular velocity is ω. The no-slip condition connects them: v = ωr, where r is the radius.

For rolling without slipping, kinetic energy has two parts: translational (½Mv²) and rotational (½Iω²). Total KE = ½Mv² + ½Iω² = ½Mv²(1 + I/(Mr²)).

The rotational part is significant. A rolling wheel has more energy than a sliding wheel of the same speed. This affects how quickly rolling objects slow down due to friction.

Socratic Questions

  1. Why does a pencil falling and spinning have its center of mass follow a parabolic path, like a simple projectile, while the pencil itself rotates chaotically?
  1. In a collision between a moving ball and a stationary one, momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is usually lost (converted to heat, sound, deformation). How can momentum be conserved while kinetic energy isn't?
  1. When an ice skater pulls in her arms while spinning, why does she spin faster? What physical quantity is conserved in this process?
  1. Why does a spinning top resist falling over? What force or torque is acting on it, and how does angular momentum explain its stability?
  1. A hoop and a disk of equal mass roll down a ramp from the same height. Which reaches the bottom faster? Why does the distribution of mass (all at the edge for a hoop, spread throughout for a disk) matter?
