Mechanical Properties of Fluids

Fluids—liquids and gases—flow and deform easily, fundamentally different from rigid solids. Yet physics governs their behavior just as rigorously.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Mechanical Properties of Fluids. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Fluids—liquids and gases—flow and deform easily, fundamentally different from rigid solids. Yet physics governs their behavior just as rigorously. Pressure in a fluid distributes force; streamlines reveal flow patterns; Bernoulli's principle connects pressure and velocity in startling ways. Understanding fluid mechanics explains why ships float, how planes fly, and why water flows from a tap. This chapter explores the physics of flowing matter.

Pressure: Force Per Unit Area in Fluids

In solids, we discussed stress (force per area). In fluids, we use pressure: the normal force per unit area.

Pressure = F/A

Pressure acts perpendicular to surfaces. Unlike solids that can withstand shear stress, fluids cannot—they flow under any shear stress. This is why fluids conform to container shapes.

Units: pascals (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m². Atmospheric pressure ≈ 10⁵ Pa. Under water, pressure increases: ΔP = ρgh, where ρ is density, g is gravity, and h is depth.

Pressure in fluids increases with depth because the weight of overlying fluid presses down. At the bottom of the ocean, pressure is hundreds of atmospheres—enough to crush an unprotected human.

Pascal's Principle and Hydraulics

Blaise Pascal discovered that pressure applied to a confined fluid transmits unchanged throughout. Pascal's principle: Pressure applied anywhere in an enclosed fluid transmits equally in all directions.

This principle powers hydraulic systems: a small force on a small piston creates high pressure; this pressure acts on a large piston, producing a large force. This is mechanical advantage through fluids.

For example, in a hydraulic lift: if the small piston has area A₁ and the large piston has area A₂, a force F₁ on the small piston produces pressure P = F₁/A₁. This same pressure acts on the large piston, producing force F₂ = P × A₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁). If A₂ is 100 times larger, F₂ is 100 times larger. A 100-pound push lifts a 10,000-pound car.

Buoyancy: Why Objects Float

Submerge an object in water, and it experiences an upward force: buoyancy. This is Archimedes' principle: A submerged object experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid it displaces.

Mathematically: Buoyant force = ρ_fluid × g × V_displaced

Objects float if buoyant force exceeds weight. For floating equilibrium, the weight of displaced fluid equals the object's weight.

This is why ships, made of dense steel, float—they displace water weighing more than the ship itself. A ship displaces volume by having hollow interiors. If you puncture the hull and water floods the interior, the displaced volume decreases, buoyant force diminishes, and the ship sinks.

Density determines floating. Objects less dense than water (cork, wood, Styrofoam) float. Objects denser than water (iron, stone) sink. The Dead Sea, extremely salty and dense, allows bodies to float effortlessly.

Streamline Flow: Smooth Fluid Motion

Streamline flow (or laminar flow) is smooth, orderly motion without turbulence. Fluid particles follow paths called streamlines—imaginary lines showing the fluid's direction.

In streamline flow:

  • No turbulence or mixing across streamlines
  • Velocity at each point is constant (steady flow)
  • Streamlines don't cross

Above a critical velocity (the Reynolds number), smooth streamline flow becomes turbulent—chaotic, with eddies and mixing. This transition is why water flowing gently from a tap becomes turbulent when the flow rate increases, breaking into splashing droplets.

Bernoulli's Principle: The Pressure-Velocity Trade-Off

Daniel Bernoulli discovered something remarkable: As a fluid's velocity increases, its pressure decreases.

Bernoulli's equation: P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant (along a streamline)

This equation balances three terms:

  • Pressure (P): static pressure
  • Kinetic term (½ρv²): dynamic pressure from motion
  • Potential term (ρgh): gravitational potential per unit volume

Along a streamline, if velocity increases, pressure must decrease to maintain the constant. If the fluid rises, kinetic energy converts to potential energy, and pressure drops.

This principle explains counterintuitive phenomena:

  • Why planes fly: Air above a wing curves and moves faster, lowering pressure above the wing. Higher pressure below pushes the wing upward (lift).
  • Why water spirals down a drain: As the spiral tightens, velocity increases, pressure decreases, and the low pressure region pulls more water inward.
  • Why shower curtains collapse inward: Fast-moving water air (outside the curtain) creates low pressure, and higher pressure inside pushes the curtain inward.

Viscosity: Fluid Resistance

Real fluids are "sticky"—they resist flow. Viscosity measures this internal friction. Honey is highly viscous; water is less so; air is nearly inviscid.

Viscous forces arise because fluid layers slide past each other. A moving object through a viscous fluid experiences a drag force:

Drag = 6πηrv (for spheres, Stokes' law)

where η is viscosity, r is radius, and v is velocity.

Viscosity increases with temperature for gases but decreases for liquids (hot oil flows; cold oil doesn't). This is why engines need different oil viscosity in winter versus summer.

In viscous flow, kinetic energy gradually converts to heat. This is why stirring honey requires effort—you're doing work against viscosity, and that energy becomes thermal energy, warming the honey.

Surface Tension

Molecules at a fluid's surface experience unbalanced forces—they're attracted inward by neighbors but have no neighbors outward. This creates surface tension: a tendency for the surface to minimize area.

Surface tension explains why water beads on waxy surfaces (the surface minimizes contact with the wax), why insects can walk on water (surface tension supports their weight), and why water droplets are spherical (spheres minimize surface area for a given volume).

Surface tension is why capillary action works: water "climbs" thin tubes against gravity because adhesion to the tube walls (stronger than surface tension) pulls water upward.

Applications: From Submarines to Flight

Understanding fluid mechanics enables engineering feats:

  • Submarines maintain buoyancy for stable underwater movement
  • Aircraft wings are shaped to maximize Bernoulli lift while minimizing drag
  • Turbines extract energy from flowing water or wind
  • Syringes use pressure to deliver medicine

Socratic Questions

  1. Why does pressure increase with depth in a fluid, but pressure from a gas in a closed container is the same at all heights?
  1. A ship made of dense steel floats, while a steel ball sinks. How is Archimedes' principle consistent with both observations?
  1. Bernoulli's principle says pressure decreases where velocity increases. But what provides the force to accelerate the fluid in the first place?
  1. Why does a shower curtain collapse inward when you turn on the hot water, even though the flowing water is moving away from the curtain?
  1. Viscosity causes friction between fluid layers. As you stir honey viscously, where does the kinetic energy go, and how can you measure the temperature increase?
