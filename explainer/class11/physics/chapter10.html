Thermal Properties of Matter

Heat and temperature govern the behavior of matter. A frozen nail is brittle; when heated, it becomes malleable.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Thermal Properties of Matter. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Heat and temperature govern the behavior of matter. A frozen nail is brittle; when heated, it becomes malleable. Bridges expand in summer and contract in winter. Food cooks when thermal energy transfers to it. This chapter explores how matter responds to temperature changes, how heat flows, and the physical foundations of thermal phenomena. Understanding these properties is essential for everything from cooking to engineering to climate science.

Temperature and Heat: A Crucial Distinction

We often use "temperature" and "heat" interchangeably in everyday speech, but physics distinguishes them sharply.

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles (atoms and molecules). It's an intrinsic property—a number describing a system's internal state. Higher temperature means faster-moving particles.

Heat is energy transferred between systems due to a temperature difference. Heat flows from hot to cold. Heat is a process, not a state property. An object doesn't "contain heat"—it contains thermal energy. Heat is the flow of that energy.

An analogy: Money in a bank account is like thermal energy. A monetary transaction is like heat—money flowing between accounts. Just as you don't say an account "contains a transaction," you shouldn't say an object "contains heat."

Temperature is measured in kelvins (K), where 0 K is absolute zero (all thermal motion stops). Celsius (°C) and Fahrenheit (°F) are convenient for daily use but scientifically inferior.

Measurement of Temperature

Thermometers rely on thermal expansion: most substances expand when heated. Mercury or alcohol in a glass tube expands, and the height indicates temperature.

More precisely, thermometers exploit any thermometric property—something that changes predictably with temperature. Examples: electrical resistance, color radiation, gas pressure, or volume.

The ideal gas thermometer defines temperature scale. An ideal gas confined at constant volume has pressure proportional to temperature: P = αT, where α is a constant. This provides an absolute temperature scale independent of specific gases or substances.

Ideal-Gas Equation and Absolute Temperature

The ideal gas law relates pressure, volume, and temperature:

PV = nRT

where P is pressure, V is volume, n is amount of gas (moles), R is the gas constant, and T is absolute temperature.

This equation reveals why absolute temperature (kelvins) is essential: at T = 0 K, pressure and volume become zero, marking the zero-point of thermal energy.

For a fixed amount of gas at constant volume: P ∝ T. For constant pressure: V ∝ T. These proportionalities break down if you use Celsius or Fahrenheit, confirming that kelvins are fundamental.

Thermal Expansion: Matter's Response to Heat

When heated, most materials expand. Thermal expansion coefficient α quantifies this:

ΔL = αL₀ΔT

where ΔL is length change, L₀ is original length, ΔT is temperature change.

Linear expansion (length change) is the easiest to measure, but materials also expand volumetrically. The volume expansion coefficient β ≈ 3α for isotropic materials.

Different materials expand at different rates. This creates problems:

  • Bimetallic strips (two metals bonded together) bend when heated because one expands more, causing stress.
  • Railroad tracks must have gaps to accommodate thermal expansion.
  • Windows can shatter if cooled too quickly—internal and external portions cool at different rates, creating stress.

Thermal expansion arises because atomic bonds are slightly asymmetric. When atoms vibrate more vigorously (higher temperature), they spend more time at larger average separations, expanding the material.

Specific Heat Capacity: How Much Energy to Heat?

Different substances require different amounts of heat to increase temperature by the same amount. Specific heat capacity (c) quantifies this:

Q = mcΔT

where Q is heat absorbed, m is mass, c is specific heat capacity, and ΔT is temperature change.

Water has a high specific heat capacity (about 4200 J/kg·K), meaning it requires substantial energy to warm. This is why water is an excellent coolant—it can absorb or release much energy with small temperature changes.

Sand has low specific heat capacity, which is why beaches get hot in the sun but cool quickly at night. The ocean warms and cools slowly due to water's high specific heat.

Molar heat capacity (heat per mole) is useful for gases. For a monatomic ideal gas at constant volume: C_V = (3/2)R. For a diatomic gas (like air): C_V = (5/2)R. The difference reflects additional rotational energy.

Calorimetry: Measuring Heat Exchange

Calorimetry measures heat transfers in isolated systems. If hot and cold objects exchange heat:

Heat lost by hot object = Heat gained by cold object

m_hot × c × (T_initial_hot − T_final) = m_cold × c × (T_final − T_initial_cold)

This principle allows measuring specific heat, determining reaction heats, or identifying substances.

The calorimeter must be well-insulated to prevent heat loss to surroundings. In reality, some heat escapes, so experimental values are slightly higher than theoretical predictions.

Change of State: Melting and Boiling

When a solid melts, its temperature remains constant despite heat input. This is because energy goes into breaking molecular bonds, not increasing thermal motion.

Latent heat of fusion (L_f) is the energy required to melt a substance at constant temperature:

Q = m × L_f

Similarly, latent heat of vaporization (L_v) is the energy to boil a liquid.

Water's latent heat of vaporization (2.26 × 10⁶ J/kg) is enormous—much larger than latent heat of fusion (3.34 × 10⁵ J/kg). This is why evaporating water cools so effectively (cooling by sweating) and why steam contains much more energy than hot water.

The reverse occurs during condensation and freezing: heat is released.

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Heat flows through three mechanisms:

Conduction: Direct molecular transfer through contact. Metals are excellent conductors (electrons carry heat); wood and plastics are poor conductors.

Convection: Bulk fluid motion carries heat. Hot air rises; cold sinks. This creates circulation. Convection is why fans cool rooms—moving air carries heat away.

Radiation: Electromagnetic waves carry heat. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation requires no medium. The sun heats Earth by radiation across the vacuum of space.

Newton's Law of Cooling

A hot object cools toward ambient temperature. The rate of cooling depends on temperature difference:

dT/dt = −k(T − T_ambient)

Solution: T(t) = T_ambient + (T_initial − T_ambient)e^(−kt)

The object's temperature asymptotically approaches ambient temperature. Half the cooling occurs in time (ln 2)/k. This law applies to coffee cooling, forensic time-of-death estimates, and industrial cooling processes.

Socratic Questions

  1. Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles. Yet, why must we use absolute temperature (kelvins) in the ideal gas law, not Celsius or Fahrenheit?
  1. Water has a much higher specific heat capacity than sand. How does this explain why coastal areas have milder climates than inland deserts?
  1. A pot of water at 100°C is boiling. You add heat, but the temperature doesn't increase. Where is the added energy going?
  1. Thermal expansion is caused by asymmetric atomic bonds. Why would atoms at higher temperature, vibrating more vigorously, spend more time at larger separations on average?
  1. Why is evaporative cooling (sweating) much more effective than cooling by conduction (touching a cold surface)? Compare latent heat of vaporization to specific heat capacity.
