Kinetic Theory

Matter is made of invisible atoms and molecules in constant motion.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Kinetic Theory. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Matter is made of invisible atoms and molecules in constant motion. This microscopic view explains the macroscopic phenomena we observe: why gases exert pressure, why temperature exists, why specific heat capacity varies among substances. Kinetic theory connects the tiny (individual particles) to the observable (pressure, temperature, density). It reveals that what we call heat is simply the chaotic motion of countless particles.

Molecular Nature of Matter

All matter consists of atoms and molecules held together by electromagnetic forces. These particles are not stationary—they vibrate, rotate, and translate constantly. This motion is thermal motion, and its average energy is what we call temperature.

The evidence for atomic structure is abundant:

  • Brownian motion: Pollen grains in water jiggle randomly, struck by invisible water molecules
  • Diffusion: Perfume spreads through a room as molecules move randomly
  • Gas behavior: Pressure arises from particles colliding with container walls

The kinetic theory is the hypothesis that macroscopic properties (pressure, temperature, heat) emerge from microscopic molecular motion. It's so successful that we now consider it established fact.

Behavior of Gases

Gases are well-described by ideal gas theory. Ideal gases have properties that make mathematical analysis tractable:

  • Particles are point masses (negligible volume)
  • Particles interact only by elastic collisions
  • Collisions with walls and other particles obey classical mechanics

Real gases deviate from ideal at high pressure (where particle volume matters) and low temperature (where intermolecular forces matter). But at normal conditions, ideal gas theory is accurate.

The ideal gas law, derived from kinetic theory, relates pressure and temperature to molecular motion:

PV = NkT (form emphasizing particle number)

where N is the number of particles, k is Boltzmann's constant (1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K), and T is absolute temperature. For one mole: PV = RT.

Kinetic Theory of an Ideal Gas

Derivation from first principles: Imagine a particle bouncing elastically off container walls. When a particle of mass m collides with a wall at velocity v, it reverses direction, transferring momentum 2mv to the wall.

If the container has area A and volume V, the time between successive collisions is Δt = 2L/v (where L is container length). The collision rate is 1/Δt.

With N particles (each contributing), the average momentum transfer per collision becomes significant, creating measurable pressure:

P = NkT/V

Rearranging: PV = NkT, the ideal gas law!

This derivation shows pressure is fundamentally molecular momentum transfer. Temperature emerges naturally as a measure of the kinetic energy of particles:

Average KE = (3/2)kT

This explains why pressure increases with temperature (molecules move faster, collide harder) and decreases with volume (fewer collisions per unit area).

Temperature as Average Kinetic Energy

The beautiful connection: Temperature is proportional to the average kinetic energy of particles.

For a monatomic gas (like helium): KE_avg = (3/2)kT

For a diatomic gas (like nitrogen): molecules also rotate, adding rotational energy. At room temperature: KE_avg = (5/2)kT

This explains why different gases have different heat capacities—more degrees of freedom (more ways to store energy) require more heat to increase temperature.

At absolute zero (T = 0), particles have minimum kinetic energy. Quantum mechanics shows they retain "zero-point energy," so they never completely stop.

Law of Equipartition of Energy

The equipartition theorem states: Each degree of freedom contributes equally to average energy.

For a gas with f degrees of freedom, the average energy per particle is:

E_avg = (f/2)kT

For a monatomic gas (3 translational degrees of freedom): E = (3/2)kT For a diatomic gas (3 translational + 2 rotational): E = (5/2)kT For a polyatomic gas (3 translational + 3 rotational + vibrational): E = (3/2)kT + more

Each translational direction contributes (1/2)kT. Each rotational direction contributes (1/2)kT. Vibrational modes (bonds stretching and bending) contribute even more because vibration has both kinetic and potential energy.

This explains why C_V varies among gases. More degrees of freedom require more heat to increase temperature.

Mean Free Path and Molecular Collisions

Molecules don't move unobstructed—they collide with each other. The mean free path (λ) is the average distance a molecule travels before colliding:

λ = 1/(√2 π d² n)

where d is the effective molecular diameter and n is number density (molecules per unit volume).

At sea level, λ ≈ 70 nanometers. In a vacuum chamber (low pressure), λ can be meters or more. In a dense liquid, molecules are nearly touching, so λ ≈ molecular diameter.

Collisions slow molecular diffusion and cause viscosity. Without collisions, molecules would move freely (high diffusion, zero viscosity). More collisions → slower diffusion, higher viscosity.

Specific Heat Capacity from Kinetic Theory

Specific heat capacity at constant volume is:

C_V = (f/2)Nk

where f is the number of degrees of freedom. At constant pressure:

C_P = C_V + Nk = [(f+2)/2]Nk

The difference C_P − C_V = Nk reflects the work done during expansion at constant pressure.

This explains why diatomic gases (like air) have C_V = (5/2)Nk while monatomic gases have C_V = (3/2)Nk. Rotation adds energy capacity.

As temperature increases, vibrational modes activate, increasing f and C_V. This explains why some materials' specific heat increases with temperature—thermal energy excites vibrational modes.

Distribution of Molecular Speeds

Molecules don't all move at the same speed. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution gives the probability of finding a molecule with speed v:

This distribution has a peak (most probable speed), mean speed, and root-mean-square (rms) speed:

v_rms = √(3kT/m)

The rms speed increases with temperature and decreases with molecular mass. Light molecules (hydrogen) move faster than heavy molecules (nitrogen) at the same temperature.

This distribution explains molecular diffusion, effusion through small holes, and molecular evaporation from liquids.

Socratic Questions

  1. Kinetic theory says pressure arises from molecular collisions with container walls. Why does pressure increase when you heat a gas at constant volume?
  1. A monatomic gas has fewer degrees of freedom than a diatomic gas. How does equipartition of energy explain why diatomic gases require more heat to achieve the same temperature increase?
  1. The mean free path is the average distance before collision. Why would mean free path be longer in a low-pressure gas than a high-pressure gas?
  1. Why can't you reach absolute zero temperature by cooling a gas? What does kinetic theory predict happens as T approaches zero?
  1. Two gases at the same temperature have different rms speeds. How does this relate to molecular mass, and why would hydrogen gas diffuse faster than nitrogen gas at the same temperature?
