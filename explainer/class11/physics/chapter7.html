Gravitation

Gravity shapes the cosmos. It holds planets in orbit, keeps our feet on the ground, and governs the life and death of stars.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Gravitation. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Gravity shapes the cosmos. It holds planets in orbit, keeps our feet on the ground, and governs the life and death of stars. Yet gravity is the weakest of nature's forces—so weak that the gravity of a small magnet can lift a paper clip against Earth's entire gravitational pull. Newton's law of universal gravitation reveals that the same force holding you down holds the moon in orbit around Earth. This profound unity illustrates physics' deepest patterns.

Kepler's Laws: Observations from the Sky

Before Newton, Johannes Kepler observed planetary motion and discovered three laws purely from data, without understanding why:

Kepler's First Law: Planets orbit the sun in ellipses, with the sun at one focus. Most planetary orbits are nearly circular, but some comets and distant planets follow highly eccentric ellipses.

Kepler's Second Law: A line connecting a planet to the sun sweeps equal areas in equal times. When a planet is close to the sun (perihelion), it moves faster. When far away (aphelion), it moves slower. This ensures the area swept per unit time stays constant.

Kepler's Third Law: The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its orbital radius: T² ∝ r³. Inner planets orbit faster (shorter periods) than outer planets. This law applies to any orbiting body—planets around the sun, moons around planets, satellites around Earth.

These laws were empirical facts with no explanation. Newton unified them with his law of universal gravitation.

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Newton proposed a radical idea: the same force that drops an apple pulls the moon toward Earth. Every mass attracts every other mass:

F = G(m₁m₂)/r²

where F is gravitational force, m₁ and m₂ are the two masses, r is the distance between them, and G ≈ 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² is the gravitational constant.

Key features:

  • Force is proportional to both masses (more mass = stronger attraction)
  • Force is inversely proportional to distance squared (r² in denominator)
  • Force is always attractive (no repulsion)
  • The force on m₁ due to m₂ equals the force on m₂ due to m₁ (Newton's third law)

The inverse-square law is profound. Double the distance, and force becomes 1/4. Triple the distance, and force becomes 1/9. This steep fall-off means gravity's range is vast (it never reaches zero) but its strength drops rapidly with distance.

The Gravitational Constant G

G is incredibly small: 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ in SI units. This is why gravity is weak. Two 1-kilogram masses separated by 1 meter experience gravitational force of only 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ newtons—undetectable without sensitive instruments.

Despite its tiny value, G is one of physics' fundamental constants, appearing in equations from planetary mechanics to stellar evolution to black holes. Its value must be measured experimentally; it cannot be derived from other principles (yet).

From Kepler to Newton

Newton showed that Kepler's laws follow mathematically from the inverse-square law. For circular orbits:

Gravitational force provides centripetal force: Gm₁m₂/r² = m₂v²/r

For orbital motion, v = 2πr/T, so:

Gm₁/r² = 4π²r/T²

Rearranging: T² = (4π²/Gm₁) × r³

This is Kepler's third law! Newton explained observations using fundamental principles.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Just as a raised object has gravitational potential energy in the potential energy section, Earth's mass exerts potential energy on objects in its field.

For objects near Earth's surface, PE = mgh (approximately constant gravity).

For objects at arbitrary distances from Earth's center:

PE = −Gm₁m₂/r

The negative sign indicates that gravity is attractive—moving objects farther apart requires work against gravity. At r → ∞, PE → 0 (chosen as the reference).

This potential energy is associated with a gravitational field—the influence Earth exerts on space around it, independent of whether another object is present.

Acceleration Due to Gravity

Earth pulls you downward with gravitational force F = GMm/R², where M is Earth's mass and R is Earth's radius.

From F = ma, the acceleration is:

g = GM/R²

This explains why g ≈ 10 m/s² near Earth's surface. It also explains why gravity is weaker on mountains (slightly larger R) and at the equator (Earth bulges outward there) versus the poles.

On other planets, g depends on their mass and radius. The moon's g ≈ 1.6 m/s² (about 1/6 Earth's) because the moon is less massive and smaller.

Escape Velocity

If you throw a ball upward, gravity pulls it back down. But if you throw it fast enough, it never falls back—it escapes Earth's gravity. This is escape velocity.

At escape velocity, kinetic energy equals the magnitude of gravitational potential energy:

½mv_e² = GMm/R

Solving for escape velocity:

v_e = √(2GM/R)

For Earth, v_e ≈ 11.2 km/s. For the moon, v_e ≈ 2.4 km/s. For a neutron star or black hole, v_e approaches the speed of light.

Escape velocity is independent of the object's mass—a feather and a boulder need the same speed to escape (though a feather requires less energy because it's lighter).

Orbital Motion and Satellite Mechanics

Objects in circular orbit have special velocity and altitude relationships. Lower orbits require higher speeds. Geostationary satellites (remaining above the same spot) orbit at Earth's equator every 24 hours at an altitude determined by Kepler's third law.

This principle enables GPS, weather satellites, and communications satellites. Understanding gravitation allows engineers to calculate precise orbital parameters.

The Deeper Meaning

Gravity isn't a mysterious force at a distance. Rather, massive objects curve spacetime itself—Einstein's insight. In this view, planetary orbits aren't "pulled" but follow straight lines through curved spacetime. This deeper theory explains phenomena Newtonian gravitation cannot, like black holes and gravitational waves. But Newton's simple inverse-square law remains remarkably accurate for most everyday purposes.

Socratic Questions

  1. Why is gravitational force so much weaker than electromagnetic force, yet gravity dominates the large-scale structure of the universe?
  1. If you're orbiting Earth in a spacecraft, you experience zero gravity. Yet Earth's gravity is still pulling you toward Earth. Why don't you feel it?
  1. Why does Kepler's second law (equal areas in equal times) follow directly from the inverse-square nature of gravity?
  1. Escape velocity depends on only a celestial body's mass and radius, not on the object escaping. Why doesn't the object's mass matter?
  1. Geostationary satellites orbit above the equator every 24 hours. How would the orbital radius change if you wanted a satellite above the North Pole with the same period?
