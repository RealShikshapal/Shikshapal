Units and Measurement

Measurement is the foundation of physics.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Units and Measurement. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Measurement is the foundation of physics. Every physical quantity—from the tiny mass of an electron to the vast distance between galaxies—must be compared against a standard reference called a unit. This chapter explores how we define these standards, express measurements with appropriate precision, and use dimensions to check whether our equations make physical sense. Without consistent units and precise measurement techniques, physics would be impossible.

Why Units Matter

Imagine trying to describe a distance without mentioning meters, feet, or any standard. You'd say "the table is this far away" while gesturing, but someone in another country wouldn't understand. Units solve this problem. A unit is simply an agreed-upon standard—like the meter for length or the kilogram for mass. Scientists worldwide use the same units so their work can be compared and verified.

The International System (SI)

Before 1960, different countries used different systems of units. The CGS (centimeter-gram-second) system was popular in some places, while others used the FPS (foot-pound-second) system. This chaos made international collaboration difficult. Today, we use the Système International (SI), which defines seven fundamental quantities:

  • Length (meter, m)
  • Mass (kilogram, kg)
  • Time (second, s)
  • Electric current (ampere, A)
  • Temperature (kelvin, K)
  • Amount of substance (mole, mol)
  • Luminous intensity (candela, cd)

Think of these as the "atoms" of measurement. All other quantities—like velocity, force, energy—are derived from these fundamentals. For example, velocity is length divided by time (m/s), and force is mass times acceleration (kg·m/s²).

Significant Figures: Telling the Truth About Precision

When you measure something, your measuring device has limits. A ruler marked in centimeters can't tell you the millimeter value. Significant figures are the digits in a measurement that are known with certainty, plus one estimated digit.

If you measure a rod as 4.5 cm using a centimeter ruler, you have two significant figures. The 4 is certain; the 5 is your best estimate. If you write 4.50 cm, you're claiming your ruler could measure to the nearest millimeter—but it can't! This overstates your precision. Significant figures communicate how trustworthy your measurement is.

When multiplying or dividing measurements, your answer can't be more precise than your least precise input. When adding or subtracting, it's about decimal places, not significant figures.

Dimensional Analysis: The Power of Checking Units

Dimensions describe what type of quantity something is—length, time, mass, or combinations thereof. They're written in brackets: [L] for length, [T] for time, [M] for mass.

A beautiful feature of physics is that equations must be dimensionally consistent. If the left side of an equation has dimensions of energy [ML²T⁻²], the right side must too. This dimensional homogeneity allows us to check whether equations make sense before testing them experimentally.

For example, the equation v = u + at (velocity = initial velocity + acceleration × time) is dimensionally correct:

  • v has dimensions [LT⁻¹]
  • u has dimensions [LT⁻¹]
  • at has dimensions [LT⁻²] × [T] = [LT⁻¹]

All terms match! If someone wrote v = u + a/t, you could immediately reject it because the dimensions don't match.

Derived Quantities and Units

From the seven fundamental units, we construct derived units for quantities like:

  • Velocity: m/s (length per unit time)
  • Acceleration: m/s² (change in velocity per unit time)
  • Force: N = kg·m/s² (mass times acceleration)
  • Energy: J = kg·m²/s² (force times distance)

Each derived unit tells you how the quantity is built from fundamentals. Understanding this structure helps you remember equations and check calculations.

Practical Applications

Dimensional analysis helps solve problems where you don't know the exact equation. If you know which quantities matter (mass, length, time, etc.), dimensional analysis can constrain what the relationship must be. For instance, if you know that the period of a pendulum depends on its length and gravity, dimensional analysis alone tells you that T ∝ √(L/g), even before deriving it from mechanics!

Socratic Questions

  1. Why do you think scientists worldwide agreed to use the same system of units? What problems would arise if different countries used different systems?
  1. If a measurement has five significant figures, what does that tell you about the precision of the measuring instrument used?
  1. Suppose someone claims that velocity equals mass plus time. How could you immediately reject this claim without doing any physics?
  1. Why is the meter defined using the speed of light rather than a physical object kept in a vault? What advantage does this give?
  1. If you know a quantity depends on mass (M) and length (L), but you don't know the equation, how could dimensional analysis help you guess the form of the relationship?

What is the dimensional formula for force?

[M L T⁻²]. Force = mass × acceleration, so M × (L T⁻²).
Significant Figures
How many significant figures does 0.00450 have?
tap to flip
Three (4, 5, and the trailing 0). Leading zeros are not significant; trailing zeros after a decimal point are.
Principle of Homogeneity
What does the principle of dimensional homogeneity state?
tap to flip
All terms added or equated in a physical equation must have the same dimensions; otherwise the equation is invalid.
Derived Unit (Energy)
What is the SI unit of energy and its base-unit form?
tap to flip
Joule (J) = kg·m²·s⁻². Energy has dimensions [M L² T⁻²].
Parallax Method
What is the parallax method used for?
tap to flip
Measuring large distances (e.g., to nearby stars) by observing the apparent shift of an object against a distant background from two viewpoints.
Absolute vs Relative Error
Define absolute error and relative error of a measurement.
tap to flip
Absolute error = |measured − true value|. Relative error = absolute error / true value, often expressed as a percentage.
