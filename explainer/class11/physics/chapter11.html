Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics governs all energy transformations involving heat.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about Thermodynamics. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Thermodynamics governs all energy transformations involving heat. It explains why perpetual motion machines are impossible, why heat engines have efficiency limits, and why some processes are reversible while others are irreversible. The laws of thermodynamics are among physics' most fundamental principles—they apply universally, from steam engines to living cells. This chapter explores the deep laws constraining all energy transformations.

Thermal Equilibrium and the Zeroth Law

Imagine two objects in contact. Heat flows until they reach the same temperature. At this point, thermal equilibrium is established—no net heat flow occurs.

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states: If object A is in thermal equilibrium with object C, and object B is in thermal equilibrium with object C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

This seems obvious, but it's profound: it defines temperature as the quantity that indicates thermal equilibrium. Two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if they have the same temperature. This law justifies the use of thermometers—a thermometer at the same temperature as an object tells us the object's temperature.

Heat, Internal Energy, and Work

A system's internal energy (U) is the total kinetic and potential energy of its particles. For a monatomic ideal gas, U = (3/2)nRT.

When heat (Q) is added to a system and the system does work (W), the internal energy changes:

ΔU = Q − W

This is the First Law of Thermodynamics: energy conservation applied to thermodynamic processes. Heat input increases internal energy; work output decreases it. If work is done on the system (compression), W is negative, and ΔU increases.

An analogy: Internal energy is like money in a bank account. Heat added is like deposits; work done by the system is like withdrawals. The change in account balance equals deposits minus withdrawals.

Applications of the First Law

Isothermal process (constant temperature): For an ideal gas, U depends only on temperature. If ΔU = 0, then Q = W. All heat input becomes work output. This seems to violate the principle that heat and work are different, but it's consistent—the system returns to its initial state, so no net internal energy change occurs.

Adiabatic process (no heat exchange, Q = 0): Then ΔU = −W. Work done by the system decreases internal energy, cooling it. Compression does work on the gas (W is negative), increasing internal energy and temperature. This is why pumping a bicycle tire heats it.

Isobaric process (constant pressure): Heat input partly increases internal energy and partly does work. Q = ΔU + W = nC_PΔT, where C_P is the heat capacity at constant pressure.

Isochoric process (constant volume): No work is done (W = 0). All heat input increases internal energy: Q = ΔU = nC_VΔT.

The Second Law: Entropy and Irreversibility

The Second Law embodies a profound asymmetry: some processes are irreversible. Heat flows from hot to cold, never spontaneously the reverse. This asymmetry isn't reflected in the fundamental laws of mechanics (which time-reverse symmetrically) but emerges from statistics.

The second law is often stated as Clausius's statement: Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot body.

Or Kelvin-Planck statement: It's impossible to extract heat from a single source and convert it entirely to work.

These equivalent statements reveal that perpetual motion machines are impossible. You cannot build a machine that converts heat entirely to work with no waste. Some heat always flows to a cold reservoir.

Entropy: The Measure of Disorder

Entropy (S) quantifies disorder or the number of molecular arrangements consistent with a system's observable properties. Higher entropy means more disorder.

For a reversible process: dS = dQ_rev/T

In an isolated system (no external energy transfer), entropy never decreases:

ΔS_universe ≥ 0

This is the Second Law in entropy form: The entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Equilibrium corresponds to maximum entropy.

An analogy: Imagine a room with books organized by author. The organized state has low entropy. If books scatter randomly, they can arrange in countless ways—high entropy. Spontaneously, books disorder; they never spontaneously organize. This reflects entropy's tendency to increase.

For a system: ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings ≥ 0. A reversible process (idealized, never achieved) has ΔS_total = 0. Real irreversible processes have ΔS_total > 0.

Heat Engines: Limited Efficiency

A heat engine absorbs heat from a hot reservoir, does work, and rejects heat to a cold reservoir. Examples: car engines, turbines, refrigerators.

An ideal reversible engine operating between hot (T_H) and cold (T_C) reservoirs has maximum efficiency:

η_max = 1 − (T_C/T_H) (Carnot efficiency)

This remarkable result (discovered by Sadi Carnot) shows that:

  • No engine can exceed this efficiency
  • Efficiency depends only on the temperatures of the reservoirs
  • To maximize efficiency, maximize T_H or minimize T_C
  • Only at T_C = 0 (absolute zero) can η = 100%

Carnot efficiency is 100% only at absolute zero, which is unattainable. Real engines are less efficient due to friction and irreversibility.

For example, a power plant with hot steam at 600 K and cooling water at 300 K has theoretical maximum efficiency of 1 − 300/600 = 50%. Real plants achieve less due to friction and heat losses.

Heat Pumps and Refrigerators

A heat pump (or refrigerator) reverses the heat engine: it extracts heat from a cold source and pumps it to a hot destination, doing work in the process.

The coefficient of performance measures effectiveness:

COP = Q_cold/W

For a reversible heat pump: COP = T_H/(T_H − T_C)

As the temperature difference increases, COP decreases—it becomes harder to pump heat against a large temperature gradient. This is why heating a house by heat pump is less efficient when it's very cold.

Availability and Irreversibility

Not all energy is equally useful. A pot of boiling water contains energy, but extracting it for work is limited by Carnot efficiency. Availability measures how much energy can be converted to useful work.

Irreversible processes destroy availability. If you run an engine less efficiently than Carnot, some energy that could have been work is converted to wasted heat. Minimizing irreversibility (through careful design) maximizes useful output.

The Third Law

The Third Law of Thermodynamics states: Entropy approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero.

S(T → 0) → 0

This implies absolute zero cannot be reached—each step closer requires infinite work.

Socratic Questions

  1. The First Law says energy is conserved: ΔU = Q − W. Yet heat and work are fundamentally different. How can energy be conserved when heat and work are distinct?
  1. Why is entropy associated with disorder? Why do scattered books never spontaneously organize into a library, yet books in a library easily scatter?
  1. A Carnot engine operates between hot and cold reservoirs. Why does its maximum efficiency depend only on the temperatures, not on the amount of heat absorbed or work performed?
  1. A refrigerator extracts heat from cold food and exhausts heat to a warm kitchen, seemingly moving heat from cold to hot (opposite to natural flow). How is this consistent with the Second Law?
  1. Why can't a car engine achieve 100% efficiency converting gasoline's chemical energy to motion, even with perfect engineering? What fundamental limit prevents this?
