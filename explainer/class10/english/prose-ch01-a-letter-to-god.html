A Letter to God

Lencho, a simple farmer, writes a letter directly to God after a devastating hailstorm destroys his crops.

Feynman Lens

Start with the simplest version: this lesson is about A Letter to God. If you can explain the core idea to a friend using everyday language, examples, and one clear reason why it matters, you have moved from memorising to understanding.

Lencho, a simple farmer, writes a letter directly to God after a devastating hailstorm destroys his crops. Desperate and faithful, he asks God for one hundred pesos to survive until the next harvest. When the postmaster and his colleagues at the post office read the letter, they are deeply moved by Lencho's unwavering faith and collect money to send him. However, due to unforeseen circumstances, Lencho receives only seventy pesos. True to his nature, Lencho interprets this as theft by the post office workers, never doubting that God heard his prayer—he simply assumes dishonest hands intercepted the divine response.

Understanding Faith and Human Nature

What is faith, and where should we place it? Faith isn't just about religious belief—it's about trust. Lencho's faith is absolute: he trusts God to solve his problems. The story explores whether faith is foolish or beautiful, naive or noble. Think of it this way: Lencho's faith is like a child fully trusting a parent. It's pure, uncomplicated, and doesn't waiver.

The conflict between faith and reality drives the story forward. Lencho experiences a real disaster—his crops are destroyed by hail. This is physical reality, something concrete and harmful. Yet his response isn't to despair or doubt; it's to write a letter to God. The postmaster and the letter carriers face their own internal conflict: they want to help Lencho, but they also want to protect his faith. They don't want him to feel abandoned by God.

The irony is powerful: The postmaster and colleagues actually perform what Lencho asked God for—they provide the money. Yet Lencho, unable to accept that humans performed a kind act, blames them for stealing thirty pesos. This reveals something crucial about human nature: when our faith is absolute, we sometimes can't accept help from ordinary people. We'd rather believe an imperfect human lied than believe God sent an incomplete answer. The story subtly asks: Is this faith admirable or destructive?

The literary technique here is situational irony—the opposite of what we expect happens. We expect the postmaster to dismiss the letter as foolish; instead, he treasures it. We expect Lencho to be grateful when he receives money; instead, he's suspicious. This irony makes us think deeper about the characters' motivations and the nature of faith itself.

Key Themes and Moral

  • Faith and trust: Lencho's unwavering faith in God, even when practical solutions are needed
  • Irony of human kindness: People help Lencho, yet he interprets their help as theft
  • Innocence and corruption: Lencho views the world in simple terms (God gives, postal workers steal), never considering human goodness
  • The limits of blind faith: When faith prevents us from acknowledging real human help and kindness

The story's moral is subtle: faith is valuable, but so is accepting help from other humans. True strength may lie in combining faith with gratitude toward those around us.

Related Concepts

Freedom and Hope • Suffering and Acceptance • Life's Simple Truths

Socratic Questions

  1. Do you think Lencho's faith in God is admirable or naive? What evidence from the text supports your view?
  1. Why does Lencho immediately assume the postmaster stole the money instead of considering that his prayer was answered, just incompletely? What does this reveal about his character?
  1. How would the story be different if Lencho had known that humans, not God, sent the money? Would it be better or worse?
  1. Is the postmaster's act of collecting money and sending it to Lencho a form of faith as well? Why or why not?
  1. What is the story really asking us about the relationship between faith, hope, and human kindness? Can all three exist together?
